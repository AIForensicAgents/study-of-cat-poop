# study-of-cat-poop

A field-ready, education-first repository for the scientific study of cat scat (droppings, feces). Practical, evidence-based notes and a curated image gallery to support biological field studies, natural-history education, and non-invasive ecological monitoring.

---

## Overview

This repository collects field observations, photographic references, and practical guidance for identifying and interpreting cat scat in the wild and urban environments. The emphasis is on **scientific rigor**, **safety**, and **ethical behavior**, while keeping the tone approachable for students, citizen scientists, and professional ecologists alike.

What you’ll find here:
- Clear morphological notes for basic classification.
- Habitat- and diet-related clues extracted from scat components.
- Practical ethics and safety guidance for field work.
- An illustrative image gallery for training and reference.

---

## Why it matters

Understanding cat scat is useful for:
- **Ecological monitoring**: domestic and feral cats impact wildlife and can be tracked indirectly through scat.
- **Dietary inference**: prey composition and urban foraging patterns can be deduced non-invasively.
- **Disease surveillance**: scat can indicate presence of parasites or pathogens (handled following biosafety protocols).
- **Citizen science & education**: a low-barrier way to teach field identification, sampling protocol, and data literacy.

Keep in mind: scat is only one line of evidence. Combine with habitat surveys, camera traps, and direct observations for robust conclusions.

## Morphology notes

Use consistent descriptors and simple measurements to maximize reproducibility.

Key morphological axes:
- **Shape & size**: typically tubular with tapered ends; measure length and maximum width (mm).
- **Consistency**: categorize as *soft*, *firm*, *dry*, or *mummified*.
- **Colour**: note fresh colours vs. aged colours (fresh = richer tones; aged = bleached).
- **Contents**: hair, feathers, bone fragments, insect chitin, plant material, seeds, parasites (visible worms or segments).
- **Odour & moisture**: record qualitatively (avoid prolonged exposure).

Suggested minimum field record (per sample):
- Date, time, GPS coordinates
- Photographs: overview + close-up with scale
- Consistency category, estimated age (fresh/stale), visible contents
- Collector initials / observer notes

Scale and lighting: include a neutral scale (ruler or coin) and note light conditions. Avoid altering a sample before photographing (no poking, prodding, or moving unless required for safety).

## Habitat / diet clues

Scat is a compact record of an animal’s recent behavior. Typical clues include:

- Hair tufts and bone fragments: indicate mammalian prey consumption (rodents, rabbits).
- Feathers or beak fragments: avian predation.
- Insect remains: urban foraging or seasonal insectivory.
- Plant fibers or seeds: scavenging or incidental ingestion (e.g., while grooming).
- Greasy or mucous appearance: may suggest a high-fat diet or digestive upset.
- Location context: proximity to dens, refuse sites, or hunting grounds refines interpretation.

Interpretation tips:
- Consider local species composition and seasonal availability of prey.
- Use multiple samples to infer patterns rather than making claims from a single droppings.
- Note human-derived items (plastic, packaging) as indicators of urban scavenging.

## Ethics / Safety

Safety and ethics are paramount. Treat all samples as potentially biohazardous and all animals (including feral cats) with respect.

Personal protective equipment (PPE):
- Disposable nitrile gloves for collection and handling.
- Hand sanitizer and/or handwashing facilities after fieldwork.
- Eye protection if disturbing aged or powdery material.

Handling and storage:
- Use sealed, labelled sample bags or tubes for any retained material.
- Avoid aerosol-generating actions (blowing, shaking) and never taste or directly sniff samples.
- Transport samples in sealed, secondary containment.

Legal & ethical considerations:
- Obtain required permits for sampling and working on private land.
- Do not trap, handle, or harass live animals without appropriate training and permits.
- Respect community norms in urban settings; prioritize non-invasive observation.

Disposal:
- Double-bag waste if disposing of contaminated consumables; follow local biohazard disposal rules.

If you suspect a zoonotic concern (e.g., unusual lesions, dead animals), contact local public health or wildlife authorities.
